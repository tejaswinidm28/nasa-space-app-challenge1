About the PACE Mission

The Plankton, Aerosol, Cloud, ocean Ecosystem (PACE) mission is a NASA Earth-observing satellite mission designed to revolutionize our understanding of the complex interactions between the ocean, atmosphere, and climate. Launched in 2024, PACE provides unprecedented data on ocean color, aerosols, and clouds, helping scientists address critical challenges such as climate change, ocean health, and air quality.

Key Objectives:

  • Study the impact of climate change on ocean ecosystems and marine life.
  • Monitor phytoplankton populations and their role in the global carbon cycle.
  • Track and predict harmful algal blooms that can affect human health and coastal economies.
  • Improve air quality forecasting by understanding the distribution and properties of aerosols.
  • Enhance our understanding of cloud formation and their role in the climate system.

Instruments on Board

Ocean Color Instrument (OCI)

OCI is a hyperspectral sensor that measures the color of the ocean with unprecedented detail. This data helps identify different types of phytoplankton, assess ocean health, and track water quality.

SPEXone Polarimeter

SPEXone is a polarimeter that measures the properties of aerosols in the atmosphere. This data helps understand how aerosols affect climate, air quality, and ocean ecosystems.

HARP2 Polarimeter

HARP2 is another polarimeter that focuses on measuring the properties of clouds. This data helps understand cloud formation, their impact on climate, and their interaction with aerosols.

Latest Discoveries

PACE Reveals New Insights into Phytoplankton Dynamics

Recent PACE observations have provided valuable data on how phytoplankton communities respond to changes in ocean currents and nutrient availability. This information is crucial for understanding the marine food web and predicting the impacts of climate change on ocean ecosystems.

Aerosol Impacts on Cloud Formation

PACE data is helping scientists unravel the complex relationship between aerosols and cloud formation. By studying how different types of aerosols influence cloud properties, researchers can improve climate models and better predict future climate scenarios.
